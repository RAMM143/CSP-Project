Detected disease is Corn Gray Leaf Spot

Corn Gray Leaf Spot

Identification:

  • The disease first appears in the form of small, necrotic spots with halos. These usually expand to become rectangular lesions, about 1/8 inch wide by up to 2 inches to 3 inches long and gray to brown in appearance. Mature lesions usually have distinct parallel edges and appear opaque when put up to the light, but the lesions hybrids vary widely in shape and color. Symptoms can sometimes be confused with northern corn leaf spot, although gray leaf spot lesions are usually limited on the sides by veins

Description:

Gray leaf spot, caused by the fungus Cercospora zeae-maydis, occurs virtually every growing season. If conditions favor disease development, economic losses can occur. Symptoms first appear on lower leaves about two to three weeks before tasseling. The leaf lesions are long (up to 2 inches), narrow, rectangular, and light tan colored. Later, the lesions can turn gray. They are usually delimited by leaf veins but can join together and kill entire leaves.


Preventive Measures


Resistant varieties: Planting corn varieties with inherent resistance to gray leaf spot is the most effective long-term prevention strategy. Research varieties suitable for your region and growing conditions.
Crop rotation: Rotate corn with non-host crops like soybeans or wheat for at least a year. This disrupts the fungal life cycle and reduces inoculum levels in the soil.
Residue management: Tillage or deep plowing can incorporate infected corn debris into the soil, where it decomposes over time. However, consider the potential downsides of tillage like soil erosion and organic matter loss. Alternatively, use crop residue shredders or compost infected debris to accelerate decomposition.
Planting practices: Improve air circulation by avoiding dense planting and maintaining proper field spacing. This helps dry leaves and reduces humidity, hindering fungal growth.
Sanitation: Remove and destroy infected plant debris after harvest to minimize the number of overwintering fungal spores.

CHEMICAL CONTROL
Resistant varieties: Planting corn varieties with inherent resistance to gray leaf spot is the most effective organic strategy. Research varieties suitable for your region and planting conditions. Fungicides: Several fungicides are registered for gray leaf spot control, including strobilurins, triazoles, and benzimidazoles. Choose products registered for corn and targeting gray leaf spot specifically.
Crop rotation: Rotate corn with non-host crops like soybeans or wheat for at least a year to reduce fungal inoculum levels in the soil. Application timing: Apply fungicides preventively before disease development or at early stages of infection (usually during tasseling and silk emergence).
Residue management: Tillage or deep plowing can incorporate infected corn debris into the soil, where it decomposes over time. However, consider the potential downsides of tillage like soil erosion and organic matter loss. Spray coverage: Ensure thorough coverage of all plant surfaces, including the lower canopy, for effective control.
Planting practices: Improve air circulation by avoiding dense planting and maintaining proper field spacing. This helps dry leaves and reduces humidity, hindering fungal growth. Follow label instructions: Always read and strictly follow the manufacturer's directions on the fungicide label regarding application rates, timings, safety precautions, and pre-harvest intervals.
Sanitation: Remove and destroy infected plant debris after harvest to minimize the number of overwintering fungal spores.
Foliar sprays: Apply organic fungicides like neem oil, potassium bicarbonate, or hydrogen peroxide. These are less effective than chemical counterparts but can offer some suppression.
Nutrient management: Maintain proper soil fertility, especially potassium levels, as balanced nutrition strengthens plant defenses against disease.

Monitor your corn plants regularly for gray leaf spot symptoms, especially during warm, humid periods.
Consider the economic threshold for your crop when deciding on control measures. This is the point where the cost of control outweighs the potential yield losses from the disease.
Integrate multiple control methods for a more comprehensive approach. Combining organic and chemical methods can enhance efficacy while minimizing reliance on chemical control alone.
Remember, organic methods may take longer to show results and require consistent application.
Remember, consulting with your local agricultural extension agent or crop advisor is highly recommended. They can provide specific recommendations tailored to your region, crop variety, and environmental conditions, ensuring effective and sustainable gray leaf spot control for your corn production.
