Detected disease is Mango Powdery Mildew

Mango Powdery Mildew

Identification:

  • On some cultivars, new flushes of growth and younger leaves are highly susceptible and may curl up and become distorted. Older leaves are more resistant to infection. Grayish, necrotic lesions or large, irregularly shaped spots may form on leaves. On very susceptible cultivars, the youngest leaves may become completely covered with fungal spores and mycelium, and eventually die . On some cultivars, the whitish residue of the fungus tends to appear on the lower leaf surface, along the leaf midrib.

Description:

Mango powdery mildew. Oidium mangiferae Berthet (a fungus), causing powdery mildew of mango, is widely distributed throughout the Mango cultivated areas. It infects panicles, fruits, and leaves. Mango is the only known host of the mango powdery mildew pathogen (i.e., only mango can be infected by the fungus).


Preventive Measures


Choose resistant varieties: If available, choose mango varieties that are more resistant or tolerant to the disease.
Plant in dry, well-ventilated areas: Grow mango trees in areas that are dry and well-ventilated.
Prune and remove weeds: Prune plants and remove tall weeds to reduce the fungus's incidence.
Intercrop with other trees: Intercrop mango trees with other tree species that are not hosts to the disease.
Avoid high nitrogen fertilization: Ensure balanced nutrition and avoid high nitrogen fertilization.
Avoid rainy areas: If possible, avoid areas that consistently have rain during the flowering season.
Use sulfur dusts and sprays: Sulfur dusts and sprays have been shown to be very effective and are exempt from tolerance.
Use overhead sprinkling: Overhead sprinkling may reduce the spread of powdery mildew because it washes spores off the plant.
Use fungicides: The first spray should be applied when the flower buds are opening, with follow-up sprays every 7–14 days until fruit set. The fungicides reported for control of mango powdery mildew include Bayleton, Benlate, and Karathane.
Alternate fungicides: Alternating different fungicides throughout a plant's season prevents the fungus from developing resistance over any of the fungicides.


CULTURAL CONTROL

CHEMICAL CONTROL
Foliar sprays containing sulfur, carbonic acid, neem oil, koanin, and ascorbic acid can prevent severe infection. Milk is also a natural fungicide that can be applied as whey to control powdery mildew. Horticultural oils such as JMS Stylet Oil, Saf-T-Side Spray Oil, Sunspray Ultra-Fine Spray Oil, or plant-based oils such as neem oil or jojoba oil can eradicate mild to heavy powdery mildew infections.

A proper nutrition builds/boosts the plants’ immunity against infections.
