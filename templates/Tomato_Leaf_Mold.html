Tomato Leaf Mold

Identification:

  • Symptoms: Tomato Leaf Mold initially appears as small, yellowish-green spots or lesions on the upper surface of the tomato leaves. As the disease progresses, these spots develop into distinctive olive-green to brown patches with a fuzzy or velvety texture. The lesions are usually confined to the upper leaf surface and may have a defined border. Infected leaves may also become distorted or curled.
  • Disease development: Tomato Leaf Mold thrives under warm and humid conditions, typically between 70째F and 80째F (21째C to 27째C) with high humidity. The spores of the pathogen spread through wind, water, or physical contact. The disease primarily affects the foliage but can also infect stems and fruits under favorable conditions.
  • Impact on yield: Severe Tomato Leaf Mold infections can lead to defoliation, reducing the plant's photosynthetic capacity and affecting fruit development and quality. This can result in decreased yield and poor overall plant health.

Description:

Tomato leaf mold is a fungal disease that affects tomato plants, particularly their leaves. It is caused by the pathogen Fulvia fulva, formerly known as Cladosporium fulvum. Preventive Measures

Plant resistant varieties: Planting tomato varieties that are resistant or tolerant to Tomato Leaf Mold is an effective approach. Look for varieties with "LM" (Leaf Mold resistant) listed in their names or consult local agricultural extension services for recommendations.


Proper plant spacing and pruning: Providing adequate plant spacing and proper pruning practices help promote air circulation and reduce humidity around the tomato plants. This can minimize the favorable conditions for disease development.

Avoid overhead watering: Watering the plants at the base, preferably through drip irrigation or soaker hoses, helps keep the foliage dry and reduces leaf moisture, which is essential in preventing disease spread.

Remove and dispose of infected plant material: Regularly monitor the tomato plants for signs of Leaf Mold and promptly remove and dispose of infected leaves or plant debris. This helps reduce the inoculum source and prevent further disease spread.

Fungicide applications: Fungicides can be used to manage Tomato Leaf Mold, particularly when disease pressure is high or when resistant varieties are not available or insufficient. Fungicides containing active ingredients such as chlorothalonil, mancozeb, or copper-based fungicides can provide control. Follow the label instructions and recommended application schedules, especially during periods of warm and humid weather.

CHEMICAL CONTROL
Crop rotation: Practicing crop rotation is crucial in preventing the buildup of Tomato Leaf Mold pathogens in the soil. Avoid planting tomatoes or other susceptible crops in the same location for consecutive growing seasons. Chlorothalonil: Chlorothalonil is a broad-spectrum fungicide that can be effective against Tomato Leaf Mold. It provides protective action by preventing the development and spread of the disease. It is typically applied as a foliar spray, and multiple applications may be necessary during periods of high disease pressure.
Proper plant spacing and pruning: Adequate plant spacing and pruning promote good air circulation and reduce humidity around the tomato plants, which helps minimize favorable conditions for disease development. This includes removing lower leaves to increase airflow and reduce potential for disease spread. Mancozeb: Mancozeb is another commonly used fungicide for Tomato Leaf Mold control. It has both protective and curative properties, inhibiting the growth and spread of the pathogen. Mancozeb is usually applied as a foliar spray and can be effective when used according to label instructions.
Mulching: Apply organic mulch, such as straw or compost, around the base of tomato plants. Mulching helps prevent soil splashing, which can spread the disease. It also maintains consistent soil moisture levels and reduces competition from weeds. Azoxystrobin: Azoxystrobin is a strobilurin fungicide that can provide control of Tomato Leaf Mold. It has both protective and curative properties, helping to manage the disease and reduce its spread. Azoxystrobin is typically applied as a foliar spray, and repeated applications may be necessary under favorable disease conditions.
Water management: Avoid overhead watering, as wet foliage can promote the development and spread of Tomato Leaf Mold. Instead, use drip irrigation or soaker hoses to water the plants at the base, keeping the leaves dry. Water in the morning, allowing the foliage to dry during the day. Propiconazole: Propiconazole is a triazole fungicide that can be effective against Tomato Leaf Mold. It has systemic properties, meaning it is absorbed by the plant and provides internal protection against the disease. Propiconazole is usually applied as a foliar spray and can provide good control when used as directed.
Organic fungicides: Several organic fungicides can be used to control Tomato Leaf Mold. These include copper-based fungicides, neem oil, and biofungicides containing Bacillus subtilis or other beneficial microorganisms. Follow the label instructions and recommended application rates for organic fungicides.
Biological control: Some beneficial microorganisms, such as Trichoderma spp., have shown potential in suppressing Tomato Leaf Mold. These biocontrol agents can be applied as soil drenches or foliar sprays. They help compete with and antagonize the disease-causing pathogens.
Organic amendments: Applying organic amendments, such as compost or well-rotted manure, can improve soil health and increase the plant's natural resistance to diseases. Organic amendments enhance soil fertility and support beneficial microbial activity.
Sanitation: Remove and dispose of infected plant material promptly. This includes infected leaves, stems, and fallen fruits. Proper sanitation reduces the spread of the disease and prevents the buildup of inoculum.
