Cassava Brown Streak CBSD Disease

Identification:

Symptoms of cassava brown streak disease (CBSD). (a) Dark brown lesions on the green portion of the stem of a severely CBSD-affected cassava plant. (b) Drying and death of axillary buds on woody stem tissue. (c) Dieback of shoot tips. (d) Feathery chlorosis. Irregular chlorosis associated with secondary and tertiary veins. (e) Dry brown necrotic rot in tuberous roots characteristic of CBSD in mature cassava plants. (f) Healthy foliage of a CBSD-resistant cultivar.

Description:

Cassava Brown Streak Disease (CBSD) is a plant disease that affects cassava plants (Manihot esculenta), which is a staple food crop in many parts of Africa. It is caused by two related viruses: Cassava Brown Streak Virus (CBSV) and Ugandan Cassava Brown Streak Virus (UCBSV).

Note:The scientific names of the viruses associated with Cassava Brown Streak Disease (CBSD) are as follows:
Cassava Brown Streak Virus (CBSV) - The scientific name for CBSV is Cassava brown streak virus.
Ugandan Cassava Brown Streak Virus (UCBSV) - The scientific name for UCBSV is Ugandan cassava brown streak virus.
These viruses belong to the genus Ipomovirus and the family Potyviridae.


Preventive Measures


1)Use disease-free planting material: Start with healthy and certified planting material from reliable sources. It is important to ensure that the planting material is free from CBSD infection. Infected cuttings can serve as a source of the disease and contribute to its spread.
2)Rogue infected plants: Regularly inspect your cassava plants for symptoms of CBSD, such as brown streaks on the stems and necrotic lesions on the storage roots. Promptly remove and destroy any infected plants to prevent further spread of the disease.
3)Maintain vector control: CBSD is primarily transmitted by whiteflies (Bemisia tabaci) and aphids (Aphis spp.). Implement measures to control these insect vectors, such as using insect-proof nets, applying appropriate insecticides (following local regulations and guidelines), or introducing biological control agents that target the vectors.
4)Plant resistant varieties: Utilize cassava varieties that have been developed or identified as resistant to CBSD. Resistant varieties can significantly reduce the impact of the disease on cassava production. Consult with local agricultural authorities, extension services, or research institutions to identify and obtain resistant varieties suitable for your region.
5)Crop rotation: Avoid planting cassava or related host plants in the same field consecutively. Crop rotation helps break the disease cycle by reducing the presence of the pathogen in the soil. By rotating cassava with non-host crops, the survival and spread of the CBSD virus can be minimized.
6)Sanitation practices: Implement good sanitation practices to minimize the buildup and spread of CBSD. Remove and destroy infected plant residues and weeds in and around the cassava fields. Proper disposal of infected plant materials reduces the chances of viral transmission and reinfection.
7)Improve overall plant health: Maintain optimal growing conditions for cassava, including proper nutrient management, balanced irrigation, and good soil health. Healthy plants are better equipped to resist and recover from diseases, including CBSD.
8)Early detection and monitoring: Regularly monitor cassava plants for symptoms of CBSD. Early detection enables timely action, such as rogueing infected plants or implementing appropriate control measures, to prevent further spread of the disease.

Management


CULTURAL CONTROL

CHEMICAL CONTROL
Use disease-resistant varieties: Planting cassava varieties that are naturally resistant or tolerant to CBSD is an effective organic control strategy. Seek out and plant varieties that have been bred for resistance or possess inherent resistance to the viruses causing CBSD. Choose appropriate insecticides: Consult with local agricultural authorities or experts to identify insecticides that are effective against whiteflies and approved for use in cassava production. Select insecticides that have a minimal impact on beneficial insects and the environment.
Clean planting material: Ensure that the planting material is free from CBSD infection. Select healthy and disease-free stem cuttings for propagation. It is advisable to use certified disease-free planting material from reliable sources. Follow instructions and safety guidelines: Read and follow the instructions on the insecticide product label carefully. Adhere to recommended rates, application methods, safety precautions, and pre-harvest intervals.
Rogueing infected plants: Promptly identify and remove cassava plants showing symptoms of CBSD. Uproot and destroy infected plants along with any nearby plants that may be affected. This helps reduce the source of infection and spread of the disease. Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Chemical control should be integrated into a broader IPM approach that includes cultural, biological, and physical control methods. IPM emphasizes the use of multiple control strategies to manage pests effectively while minimizing reliance on chemical treatments.
Cultural practices: Implement cultural practices that enhance plant vigor and promote overall plant health. This includes maintaining proper soil fertility, optimal irrigation practices, and providing adequate spacing between plants to improve air circulation and reduce disease incidence. Timing and frequency: Apply insecticides at the appropriate timing to target the whitefly life cycle stages when they are most vulnerable. Depending on the severity of the whitefly infestation, multiple applications may be necessary.
Vector control: Whiteflies are known to transmit the viruses causing CBSD. Implementing measures to manage whitefly populations can help reduce disease transmission. This can involve the use of physical barriers, such as insect-proof nets, to prevent whitefly infestation, or the application of organic insecticides approved for use in organic farming.
Biological control: Explore the use of biological control agents to manage whitefly populations. Beneficial insects like predators and parasitoids can help naturally suppress whitefly populations. Consult with local experts or agricultural extension services to identify suitable biological control agents for your region.
Crop diversification and rotation: Practice crop diversification by intercropping cassava with other crops that are not hosts for CBSD. This helps disrupt the disease cycle and reduce the buildup of the viruses in the field. Additionally, avoid consecutive plantings of cassava in the same area to minimize the disease's impact.
Soil management and organic amendments: Maintain soil health through organic practices such as incorporating organic matter, compost, or well-decomposed manure into the soil. Healthy soils support robust plant growth and enhance the plant's ability to resist diseases.
