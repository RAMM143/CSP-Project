Rice Leaf Blight

  • Symptoms: The initial symptoms of Rice Leaf Blight include the appearance of water-soaked lesions on the leaves. As the disease progresses, the lesions enlarge and turn grayish-white or light brown. They may have a rectangular or elliptical shape and can coalesce, leading to larger blighted areas on the leaves. In severe cases, the lesions may expand to cover the entire leaf surface.
  • Lesion development: The lesions on the leaves may exhibit a characteristic angular shape, with distinct boundaries defined by leaf veins. The lesions may also have a yellow halo surrounding them.
  • Disease progression: Rice Leaf Blight can spread rapidly under favorable conditions, such as high humidity and warm temperatures. The disease can affect all above-ground parts of the rice plant, including leaves, leaf sheaths, and panicles.
  • Impact on yield: Severe infections of Rice Leaf Blight can cause significant yield losses in rice crops, primarily due to reduced photosynthetic activity and premature leaf senescence. Additionally, the disease can weaken the plants and make them more susceptible to other pathogens.

Preventive Measures

BIOSECURITY :
National biosecurity organisations need to ensure that rice seed entering the country originates from crops certified free of bacterial leaf blight and is subject to closed quarantine upon arrival, and further testing for the bacterium causing this disease. Note there are different strains in different parts of the world.

Management


CULTURAL CONTROL

CHEMICAL CONTROL
In low-input farming systems, where resource-limited farmers can hardly ever afford external inputs, control of bacterial leaf blight is mainly through the use of resistant/tolerant varieties in combination with sound management practices, such as good weed control. Copper-based fungicides: Copper-based fungicides, such as copper hydroxide or copper oxychloride, can provide control of Rice Leaf Blight. These fungicides have a protective action and are typically applied as foliar sprays.
Before planting:
Take care when selecting seed. For farmers who are using their own seed, it is important that they always select from plants uninfected by bacterial leaf blight symptoms. 		Mancozeb: Mancozeb is a broad-spectrum fungicide that can be used to control Rice Leaf Blight. It is often used as a tank mix partner with other fungicides to enhance disease control and manage resistance.
During growth:

  • Do not damage seedlings when taking them from the nursery. They should not be pulled from the soil but eased out with roots intact; the leaves should not be clipped.

  • Be careful with fertilizer applications: too much nitrogen increases shoot and leaf production and creates conditions that promotes disease development because of higher humidity. A suggested amount is 80-100 kg N/ha, depending on local conditions.

  • Ensure good drainage of fields (and nurseries). Most varieties are not able to withstand flooding for more than a week, and floodwater spreads the bacterial leaf blight between plants.

  • Keep field clean of weeds, especially species of Leersia which are considered to be the main weed host of bacterial leaf blight.
Triazoles: Fungicides belonging to the triazole group, such as tebuconazole, propiconazole, and difenoconazole, have shown effectiveness against Rice Leaf Blight. These fungicides have both protective and curative properties.
After harvest:
  • Plough land to bury rice stubble, straw, ratoon plants and volunteer seedlings. Note that the bacteria causing this disease do not live long in the soil.
  • Allow fallow fields to dry to destroy the bacterium in the soil and in plant remains.
Strobilurins: Fungicides from the strobilurin group, such as azoxystrobin and pyraclostrobin, can provide control of Rice Leaf Blight. These fungicides have protective and anti-sporulant properties.
RESISTANT VARIETIES :
  • Resistant varieties are available in Southeast Asia: PSB Rc82 is a standard variety with resistance; Macassane, has been shown to have improved bacterial leaf blight resistance and is being used in Mozambique, and two IRRI varieties, IR22 and IR 54, are resistant and moderately resistant, respectively, in Tanzania. Several NERICA hybrids from AfricaRice have been tested and found to have resistance. Check whether these are available locally.
Mixtures and formulations: Some fungicides are available as mixtures or formulated products that combine different active ingredients for broader spectrum control and improved efficacy against Rice Leaf Blight.


"Regular monitoring of rice fields, adherence to recommended management practices, and early intervention are crucial for effectively managing Rice Leaf Blight.
